First-timer's guide

How renting in New York actually works.

No jargon, no pressure. Here's what to expect from first search to signed lease — and exactly what to have ready so nothing slows you down.

Figure out your real budget

Most NYC landlords want your annual income to be about 40 times the monthly rent. So a $3,000 apartment usually needs roughly $120,000 in household income. Don't have that solo? A guarantor or a roommate can close the gap — more on both below.

Understand fees up front

Some listings are "no fee," meaning you don't pay a broker commission. Others carry a fee (often around one month's rent or up to ~15% of annual rent). Beyond that, budget for first month, a security deposit (capped at one month in NY), and possibly last month. We flag no-fee listings clearly so there are no surprises.

Get your paperwork ready before you tour

Good apartments are gone in days, so have your packet ready: photo ID, recent pay stubs, a letter of employment, last year's tax return or W-2, and recent bank statements. Having this set means you can apply the same day you fall in love with a place.

Know what a guarantor is

A guarantor co-signs your lease and agrees to cover rent if you can't. They usually need to earn about 80× the monthly rent and live in the tri-state area. No personal guarantor? Institutional services (like Insurent or The Guarantors) can act as one for a fee.

Tour smart, then apply fast

See places in person when you can — light, noise, and the walk to the train don't show up in photos. When one fits, apply quickly and completely. A clean, full application beats a higher offer that's missing documents.

Application, approval, and lease signing

The landlord runs credit and background checks and reviews income. Once approved, you'll review and sign the lease and pay your move-in costs. Read it closely — renewal terms, sublet rules, and what's included (heat, hot water, etc.). Then you get the keys.
